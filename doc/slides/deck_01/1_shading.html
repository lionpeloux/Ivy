methodology
I would like to introduce a formalism for the purpose of parametric studies. This formalism is a methodological effort and contribution to the field of parametric design and analysis.
In such a study a system is analyzed regarding several variables named pa- rameter. Each parameter can vary in a discrete or continuous range of scalar values. If not, the modeling of the system can easily be reinterpreted in that way. For instance a (x,y,z) point parameter can be understood as 3 scalar parameters.
States of a parameter are modeled as a finite and strictly ordered set :

$S=\{s_1< s_2<~...< s_{N}\}$

$s_i \in \mathbb{R} ~~,~~ i \in [1,N]$

A grid is the cartesian product of several sets :

$G = S_1 \times S_2 \times \cdots \times S_{D}$

$ \begin{aligned} S_j &=\{s_1^j< s_2^j<~...< s_{N_j}^j\} \\ N &= N_1 \times N_2 \times \cdots \times N_{D} \end{aligned} $

A node is a grid vertex :

$g_{i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D}} = (s_{i^1}, s_{i^2}, ..., s_{i^{D}})$

$s_{i^j} \in S_j ~~|~~ i \in [1,N_j] ~~,~~ j \in [1,D]$

A node is identified its a unique tuple address :

$ i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D} \Leftrightarrow (i^1,i^2,\cdots,i^{D}) $

$i^j \in [1,N_j]$

There are many ways to order a list of N nodes

$N!$

N! to be exact.

Not all ways are made equals in terms of convenience.

Especially when it comes to defining cells.

explain how nodes are numbered. For 3 dimension. The grid is a cube but is more generally a hyperrectangle. Cells are not cubes but parralelepiped.

$ \begin{array}{r|cccc} Node & i^1 & i^1 & i^2 & \cdots & i^{D} \\ \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 2 & 2 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 3 & 3 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ N_1 & N_1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \end{array} $

$ \begin{array}{r|cccc} N_1 & N_1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ +1 & 1 & 2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ +2 & 1 & 2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ +3 & 1 & 2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 2*N_1 & N_1 & 2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \end{array} $

$ \begin{array}{r|cccc} 2*N_1 & N_1 & 2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ (N_2-1) * N_1 & N_1 & N_2-1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ +1 & 1 & N_2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ +2 & 2 & N_2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ +3 & 3 & N_2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ N_2*N_1 & N_1 & N_2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \end{array} $

$ \begin{array}{r|cccc} N_2*N_1 & N_1 & N_2 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 2*(N_2* N_1) & N_1 & N_2 & 2 & \cdots & 1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 3*(N_2* N_1) & N_1 & N_2 & 3 & \cdots & 1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ N_3*N_2* N_2 & N_1 & N_2 & N_3 & \cdots & 1 \end{array} $

$ \begin{array}{r|cccc} N/N_D & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & N_D \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ N_D-1\times \cdots \times N_{1} & N_1 & N_2 & N_3 & \cdots & N_D-1 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ N-N_1 & 1 & N_2 & N_3 & \cdots & N_D \\ +1 & 1 & N_2 & N_3 & \cdots & N_D \\ +2 & 2 & N_2 & N_3 & \cdots & N_D \\ +3 & 3 & N_2 & N_3 & \cdots & N_D \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ N & N_1 & N_2 & N_3 & \cdots & N_D \end{array} $

Sorting the nodes is achived by a recursive algorithm.

Nodes can now be indexed :

$ \begin{array}{rl} i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D} & \Leftrightarrow (i^1,i^2,\cdots,i^{D}) \\ & \Leftrightarrow n_{i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D}} \end{array} $

$n_{i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D}} \in [1,N]$

Nodes can be indexed from their tuple adress :

$ \begin{array}{rrl} n_{i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D}} & = & 0 \\ & += & i^1 \\ & += & i^2 * N_1 \\ & += & i^3 * N_2 * N_1 \\ & & \cdots \\ & += & i^{D} * N_{D-1} * \cdots * N_1 \end{array} $

$i^j \in [1,N_j] ~~,~~ j \in [1,D]$

Recursive euclidean division leads back to the address :

$ n_{i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D}} = i^1 + N_1 * (i_2 + N_2 * (i_3 + ... + N_{D-2} * (i_{D-1} + N_{D-1}*i_D))) $

$ \left \{ \begin{aligned} i^0 &= 0 \\ q^0 &= n \\ \\ i^1 &= q^0\;mod(N1) \\ q^1 &= q^0 : N1 \\ \\ i^{j} &= q^{j-1} \;mod(N_j) \\ q^j &= q^{j-1} : N_j \end{aligned} \right. $

We now have a robust way to construct and browse a grid from its nodes. We can look at the construction of it cells.

Constructing cells matters as we want to provide interpolation of data at any point in the grid ; not only at nodes.

A cell is defined by its corner nodes.

It can be seen as hyperrectangle which is a convex polytope.

Topologically a cell is also a grid with the minimal possible number of nodes.

The number of cells in a grid is :

$N_C = (N_1-1) \times (N_2-1) \times \cdots \times (N_{D}-1)$

The number of corner nodes in a cell is :

$2^D$

Each cell is indexed relativelly to its origin node :

$ n_{i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D}} \rightarrow c_{i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D}} $

$i^j \in [1,N_j-1] ~~,~~ j \in [1,D]$

Like nodes, cells can be indexed regarding their tuple address.

$ i^1i^2~\cdots~i^{D} \Leftrightarrow (i^1,i^2,\cdots,i^{D}) $

$i^j \in [1,N_j-1]$

Corner nodes ar obtained by a set of permutations about the origin :

$ \begin{array}{r|cccc} Origin & i^1 & i^1 & i^2 & \cdots & i^{D} \\ \\ & + & + & + & \cdots & + \\ \\ k & \sigma_k(i^1) & \sigma_k(i^1) & \sigma_k(i^3) & \cdots & \sigma_k(i^D) \end{array} $

$\sigma_k(i^j) \in \{0,1\} ~~,~~ i^j \in [1,N_j-1] ~~,~~ j \in [1,D] ~~,~~ k \in [1,2^D]$

Explain corner nodes and permutations

$ \begin{array}{r|cccc} Origin & i^1 & i^1 & i^2 & \cdots & i^{D} \\ & + & + & + & \cdots & + \\ 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 3 & 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 4 & 1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 5 & 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ 6 & 1 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ 7 & 0 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ 8 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 2^D & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \end{array} $

The grid is now fully described with a finite number of nodes and cells. It is a well organized space where each state has it's own place.

This gives a solid basis to :

data structure to store analysis results

browse the results efficiently for analysis and visualization

interpoate results at any point in the grid space

Interpolation
Lets consider a cell defined by its corner nodes :

$ \begin{aligned}{} g_{0000...0} & = (s_0^1,s_0^2,s_0^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ g_{1000...0} & = (s_1^1,s_0^2,s_0^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ g_{0100...0} & = (s_0^1,s_1^2,s_0^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ g_{1100...0} & = (s_1^1,s_1^2,s_0^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ g_{0010...0} & = (s_0^1,s_0^2,s_1^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ g_{1010...0} & = (s_1^1,s_0^2,s_1^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ g_{0110...0} & = (s_0^1,s_1^2,s_1^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ g_{1110...0} & = (s_1^1,s_1^2,s_1^3,s_0^4,\cdots,s_0^D) \\ & \\ g_{1111...1} & = (s_1^1,s_1^2,s_1^3,s_1^4,\cdots,s_1^D) \end{aligned} $

Lets consider a point in that cell :

$X = (x_1,x_2,x_3,\cdots,x_D)$

$x_j \in [s_0^j,s_1^j] ~~,~~ j \in [1,D]$

This point partition the cell into a number of sub-cells :

$ 2^D $

Lets define the volume of the cell as :

$$ V = \prod_{j\in[1,D]} (s_1^j-s_0^j) $$

Lets define the volume of a sub-cell as :

$$ V_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}}(X) = \prod_{j\in[1,D]} \small \begin{cases} s_1^j-x_j,& \sigma_j=0 \\ x_j-s_0^j,& \sigma_j=1 \end{cases} $$

Linear interpolation factors are given by :

$$ L_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}}(X) = \frac{1}{V} * V_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}}(X) $$

A scalar field that is known at each node can thus be interpolated :

$$ f(X) = \prod_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}} f_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}} * V_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}}(X) $$

$f_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}} = F(g_{\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3\cdots\sigma_{D}})$
